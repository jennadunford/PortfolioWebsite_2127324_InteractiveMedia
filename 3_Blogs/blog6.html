Defining The Internet

The world that we live in is no longer a purely physical space. All around us, basic human interactions are migrating to digital spheres. The onset of the Covid-19 pandemic has caused this "digital migration" to rapidly grow, we witnessed the world change before our very eyes.

Integral parts of society started to exist more and more online. As we cautiously take steps towards leaving our pandemic-stricken state of living - a lot of these online digital spaces will remain. New norms have formed. You could say that the world has changed, but it is more nuanced than that. The digital world has grown, it has tightened its grip on our lives, and the physical world has been beaten down by the pandemic and the following economic crisis. Those who were unable to fully migrate into these new online spaces were left in the dust of progress.

We cannot define the concept of the internet now the same way we would have pre-2020. It is no longer simply a place to connect, to find and transmit information. The internet is so much more now. The internet is an important organ of the modern humans societal "body". With the internet, people join educational platforms, people find jobs, people work their jobs, businesses operate, commerce exists, and people access resources for health. A person left now without access to the digital world is left in darkness.

In this brave new digital world, the internet is a basic human right - alongside shelter, water, and health. Without it, how do we expect someone to fully participate and pursue happiness in our society.

Defining Society

We can define society by the rules, conventions, and systems that form the structure of our daily lives. The digital world itself is a system that forms part of people's lives now, and so it makes up our society today.

The implication that the digital space is a part of modern society means that, due to the unequal access to technology and internet, there is active exclusion of marginalized individuals and groups from modern society. Exclusion from society is a dangerous situation for any individual. Resources, information, education, healthcare, the job market all exist in some shape or form online. To individuals who have always had access to the internet, it seemed necessary to begin to move so many of these things online, and it is clear not much thought was given to the people who would be left behind.

A large part of our society exists in the digital space. This is undeniable. And so it is also undeniable that this digital migration to online spaces has left some people unable to fully and meaningfully participate in society. This concept sounds like the plot of a dystopian sci-fi story, but it is now a reality.

It is not only the internet that makes up part of our society, it is also the technologies that come with it. Algorithms that read in human behavior and then classify individuals make up a structure in modern society as well. Algorithms, lines of code, have the power to label and identify people by their gender, the color of their skin. Algorithms can determine which people are shown job listings in various fields, they can also determine whether or not to label individuals as dangerous based on the inherent discriminatory practices in our society.

Although it was thought that the onset of the digital age would bring down the barriers that prevent people from accessing information and sharing their voices, the technology has only brought along new ways to exacerbate already existing prejudices and discrimination. Without addressing and destroying the core problems at the very root of our modern society, technology has simply adapted to them. It could even be argued that some parts of technology have made these problems even worse.

This new society with the internet and digital spaces embedded within it has great power. It has the power to connect people to valuable resources, to bring different people and cultures together, but there is also a great burden now. There is the burden of trying to make sure that this technology is not misused, and to ensure that it accessible to all, not only accessible to the already privileged.

Defining Design Justice

And now, what is design justice? We need justice for marginalized individuals and communities in terms of accessibility to technology, as well as preventing discrimination due to technology.

Technology does not exist in a vacuum, and it is not a thing of nature. Technology will always be a product of the people that design and develop it. Although there is a perception that technology and algorithms are inherently neutral, any biases held by the designers and developers will be communicated in the design of these technologies. Here lies the problem. There is a broad lack of accountability that exists amongst the people making this technology. Marginalized groups are also desperately underrepresented in the tech community.

When it comes to undoing and repairing the damage done by oppression, it cannot be the oppressors doing the repairing. Solutions to the problems that impact marginalized groups cannot be created without those marginalized individuals and communities.

Design justice. Designing technology for justice. Designing technology that dismantles discrimination, designing technology while being extremely mindful of the power that it has, and the biases that it could contain.

Design justice includes accessibility. There can be no design justice for people if those people cannot access the technology in the first place. Allof these problems are vastly intersectional in nature, all existing together, and they must all be dealt with together as well. There cannot be only justice for one group, one community, one issue, design justice must be done for all and for every existing problem.

This is a huge implication, and although small steps towards a just digital society are good, it is important for us as designers and developers to remember that the work will never truly be finished. Not until there is global access to the digital world, and access without discrimination embedded within it.

And the work of design justice will never truly be done either. Society is not a static concept, our world and our mindsets are forever changing. We have seen just how fast our daily lives can be changed after living through a global pandemic.

With this being said, I think that the conclusion can be made that design justice is not a goal or a target. Design justice should be a mindset, a strategy, a constant and ever-present force driven by the people in communities of technology and programming. We do not strive for some final goal of design justice, we must strive for design justice to be ever-present and evident in our work and our designs. And there can be no design justice without the people who need the justice deeply involved with it. We need accessibility, we need people willing to give voices to marginalized people, and we must allow affected communities to take up more spaces within the structures that discriminate against them so that those structures can be dismantled.

Internet, society, and design justice. These are complex issues, but they are all human issues, and our way of life is deeply dependent now on all of them.
