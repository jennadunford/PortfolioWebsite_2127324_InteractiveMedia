1. Introduction

Relying on a breakdown of the text, "Towards a Study of Information Technologies", this report will discuss the aims, methodology, results, and the conclusions of the text. Additional research will also be done to compare, analyse, and discuss differences and similarities between other pieces of literature on the subject. For the scope of the report, the entire text should be broken down as concisely as possible, and a required amount of other pieces of research needed to understand the different thought methodologies of this field will be used and referenced. This report is intended to provide a critical discussion and analysis of the problems presented.

2. Breakdown of Text

2.1 Text Introduction: Information Geographies and (im)mutable Augmentations

[1] The introduction to the text introduces the concept that information has geography, meaning information will always be somehow connected to places and between places. Information also always exists physically, whether on books and paper or on internet servers. Information also moves. The article emphasizes that these conventions for information have not changed in the digital age, the core principles of information mentioned before still very much apply to the world today. The principle of immutable information means that when information is transported between places there is no significant change to the core meanings of the information.

In the digital age, technology has now facilitated a concept that the article calls (im)mutable augmentations, where information is layered dynamically across geographic spaces, and this information itself becomes changed and moulded and has influences on the geography where the information originated. The information is mutable and immutable. This (im)mutable augmentation of information allows people to access geographic and other forms of information during geographic activities, i.e. travelling through spaces. The consequence of this is that places that have not been catalogued in internet spaces can become invisible to people. The way places are represented on the internet can also affect how they are viewed by people. The emergence of geographic information being unleashed into the digital age has made geographic information more than simply a representation of space. The geographic information and how it is presented can and will have direct, material effects on the places represented, and unrepresented.

The article states that it intends to explore information geographies and their distributions, access, participation, and representation.

2.2 Pre-Digital Pieces of Information

Before the digital age information was scarce and very geographically dependent. Tools for the creation and distribution of information collections were held by small groups of people. Large-scale data collection was rare. Books, where information was largely stored, had limited access for the general public and there was limited access to their production. Access to information was also controlled by the formats that were chosen to display information, that could only be understood by those with the appropriate training.

Another hindrance to information accessibility were the required skillsets needed to create, organize, catalogue, and store information on a large scale. Due to this need, institutions such as universities were created, however, access to these institutions would be limited still to most people.

Due to power relations around the world, it was more common for information to be created and accessed in the global North while the global South suffered a vast lack of access to information and the creation of information. Information was very much controlled by power, systems of governance, and global areas of inequality.

2.3 Changing Geographies after the Information Revolution

In recent years, the dawn of the digital age, there have been large changes in the availability of information. These changes have been created through the increased access to computers and the internet which allows people with access to view, create, share and organize information on a large scale. In essence, many barriers that existed in the pre-digital era of information have been removed.

There has been a lot of talk about how the digital age has removed all restrictions on information, however, this is not such a simple idea. Just because there are ways to have basically unlimited access to information, does not mean that this exists for everyone. People and information are still restricted and bound by geographical circumstances. What would be a good method of investigating this and finding a way to prove that geographies of the global population changes how information is created, accessed and shared?

The article suggests that three topics be explored, researched and discussed in detail:

  1. Geographies of Access and Enablement
  2. Geographies of Participation
  3. Geographies of Representation

All of these topics will be mapped out using data sets, and then discussed. The article notes that the methods of data collection will not be a wholly accurate representation of information geographies across the world, due to limitations in how data can be found at this point in time. However, the information found should still be of value in painting a general picture of the current state of information geographies around the world.

The article also raises the point that this research will create more questions than answers about global information geographies, however, this is the intention of the article. The authors want this paper to encourage more members of the academic community to explore, research, collect data and answer questions about this topic.

2.4 Geographies of Access and Enablement

The article tells us that understanding the geographies of access and enabled will provide insights into the distribution of various technologies that are essential for people to engage with digital information.

Internet access is the most important aspect of gaining access to digital information, therefore, it is important to illustrate world-wide patterns of connectivity. The article used data from the World Back that tracked internet users worldwide to create a mapped diagram showing where most internet users are situated.

The data shows that the global North has overwhelmingly more internet users than the global South, displaying a lack of access to digital information for most of the world population.

To further analyse the global distribution of internet users, the article notes another important aspect of digital accessibility, broadband affordability. The authors collected data from the International Telecommunication Union on the price of internet connection as a percentage of the average income of each country.

The data was displayed on a map which showed that the global South had disproportionally high broadband prices compared to the global North. With regards to average salaries, it is much more expensive to access the internet in the global South, indicating a possible reason as to why internet users are so few in this part of the world.

2.5 Geographies of Participation

An important part of access to digital information is participation in the creation and sharing of that information. The article uses three variables to measure the worldwide distribution of internet participants.

The first variable is domain names, where the authors investigated the distribution of domain names by country, it was found that the United States and Europe has the highest percentage of domain names linked to their countries, again showing that the global North has a large, unequal share of internet participation in terms of domain names. The article states that a reason for this could be the regulations around domain names in various countries, in some countries there are stricter guidelines around registering domain names, which may prevent many internet users from accessing them.

The second variable is distributions of collaborative coding, this article used GitHub to collect data, the largest platform for internet users to publicly publish and collaborate on software development. Using the data available, the authors discovered that countries in the global North had far greater GitHub commits than the global South. This again shows that even the few with internet access in countries with low internet populations, those few do not engage with digital information on the same level that countries with high internet populations do.

The third variable is the geography of Wikipedia contributions because Wikipedia is the largest encyclopaedia and allows all internet users to participate in information contributions and edits. Wikipedia also supports may different languages, allowing more accessibility. The Wikipedia contributions data shows that the continent of Africa as a whole as very few entries, exampled by the fact that Hong Kong alone has more Wikipedia edits than the entirety of Africa, this again displays the inequalities around internet access in Africa and the global North.

2.6 Geographies of Representation

Geographies of representation aims to show which parts of the world are represented or underrepresented in the digital world. The article uses data from Google searches, OpenStreetMap and the Semantic Web to study this.

The authors used Google search data to find which country names had the most results brought up. The analysed data reiterated the inequalities seen in earlier analyses that the article had conducted and shown.

OpenStreetMap is the largest map of roads in the world, this is because of its open-source nature. Seeing which areas and places had the most comprehensive road data shows which areas are properly represented geographically and which countries are less represented. Again, the analysed data showed that the global North has much more geographical representation.

The semantic web refers to actions taken to make the internet as a whole more accessible with data than can be reused easily on various services. This article focuses on the services of Freebase and Geonames. Freebase is a general knowledge database with millions of entries and Geobase is the largest directory of place names. Taking the data from these two services and mapping them out again shows the disparity shown in the previous analyses. There is much more representation in terms of digital information and representation in the global North than in the global South.

2.7 Conclusions: Towards a Study of Information Geographies

From the review of geographies of digital access seen in the paper, it is clear that the view that the internet makes information accessible to everyone is a false one. There are very large parts of the world with little to no stake in the world of digital information access, contribution, and representation. Many facets of our society need to be addressed to fix these gaps in information geographies including attention to policy-making, post-colonial information governance and the infrastructures that are in place that limit digital access to large parts of the global population.

This paper aims to encourage more research to be done into the data displayed and discussed so that the global community can better understand the issue at hand and find ways to make digital information geographies truly accessible to all.

3. Aims

The aim of the summarized article was to conduct never-before-done research into the geographies of digital information in order to bring more understanding to the fact that there are large areas of our world where the concept of the internet being open and accessible to all does not exist. This is not a subject where much research was done before, and so this article also aims to open a new area of study in order to gain clearer understandings of the problems discussed in the paper.

Another aim was to graphically show the disparity in digital information access, it's hard to relate plain numbers to the world, however showing the data depicted in maps shows the vast scale of information disparities. This is something that was not formally done before, according to the article.

4. Methodology

The methodology of the data collection in the article involved gathering information from various organizations and organizing the information so that it could be displayed geographically, or in a way to show relative size.

The methodology used was effective, as it displayed the disparities in digital information access on a global scale in a digital manner, and graphically, which was also effective. However, the article does admit that this collection of data was nowhere near extensive enough and could not display smaller nuances in digital access across the world using only generalized datasets. In future research, more data should be collected at local scales, to better understand the smaller factors that influenced access to information digital across the world.

5. Results and Conclusions

The results of the research conducted in the article showed, overwhelmingly, that there a global disparity in digital information access. The global South, which has historically been a place of inequality, does not have the same level of access, contribution, or representation of digital information that the global North enjoys.

These results were predictable, but it is an important step towards a more equal level of global information distribution, the more the issue is studied, displayed, and understood, the more that can be done to find ways to mitigate and resolve the causes of these disparities.

6. Review of Additional Literature

What this article describes as “disparities of global information geographies” I was taught about under a different name, the “digital divide”. In High School level Information Technology and in Geography, the syllabus included sections explaining the digital divide as a concept describing how some people have access to the internet and other digital technologies, and some people, because of various factors, do not have this access. What's interesting is that this paper focuses on the issue on a global scale, but locally, in a South African context, we can see the digital divide right on our doorsteps. Just kilometres away from our schools were areas with very little access to high-quality digital technology and internet access. This is not simply an issue that can be analysed using shades of colour on world maps, it is something that should be pointed out and seen in our own country and communities. This first paragraph has just been talking about my own personal experience, not a literature review, so I don't have a source for it, but I do believe that my own view on the subject is important as well.

The term “Information Geographies” as used in the article is not used very commonly among scholars it seems, according to a quick search through Google Scholar, but I did find a couple of useful articles to review about the geographical nature of the digital divide. I read an article written by Mark Graham, who was also an author in the article that was just summarized, called “Warped Geographies of Development: The Internet and Theories of Economic Development”[2], which talks about many of the same subjects as in the paper first reviewed, however, the information is not as well displayed as it is in the diagrams and maps as in the first article I summarized. This article does do a good job of introducing the ideas, allowing them to be explored further.

Another article that discusses a similar idea: “Segueways into Cyberspace: multiple geographies of the digital divide” by Barney Warf [3]. It starts by stating the fact that we need to remove the idea that the internet and its accessibility removes “geography” as a whole and makes us one global community with equal internet access. The article talks about the global elite controlling the power dynamics of access to technology, how all the factors of class, race, gender, religion and most importantly, spatial disparities (i.e Information Geographies), severely impacts the accessibility of digital information for much of the world. The paper analyses datasets of internet users and internet hosts across the world, using world maps to display the data in a useful manner. In the original article, Graham et al says that this is not an area that is very much discussed, however this article, published before the one summarized earlier, was published in 1999, revised in 2000, the original manuscript for “Towards a Study of Information Geographies” was published in 2015. It is clear that there were scholars concerned about the study of this issue before the conceiving of the topics in the original paper.

In fact, “Segueways into Cyberspace” explores the issue down to the more nuanced factors of racial, gender and other inequalities and how they affect digital information accessibilities. The intersectionality presented in the article is important when discussing the digital divide because they can provide a better framework for finding solutions to these disparities. However, this article focuses on those specific issues in the context in the United States, which is an easier task than trying to take on those issues in the context of the entire global population.

Another article, written in 2010: “Theorizing Digital and Urban Inequalities: Critical geographies of 'race', gender and technological capital” by Melissa Gilbert [4], addresses similar issues in the original article. This article also has a focus on the United States, it analyses the disparities and inequalities of access to digital technology in economically marginalized urban residents. It proposes that this view of the issue will better inform future frameworks for internet access for people who suffer from the inequalities in urban areas. This article does not specifically take on the information geographies concept, as in the original article, but it does do a good job of explaining the smaller nuances of the issue on a very local scale. It does this by examining the real-life experiences and views of people directly impacted by the digital divide.

7. Analysis of Findings

I found that despite the claims made in “Towards a Study of Information Geographies”, the data analysed in the article had been analysed before, and it was a topic interested in by several scholars, some with even more nuanced views on the factors revolving around Information Geographies.

However, in saying this, some of the methods used were unique, such as analysing data around information access, contribution and well as representation. Perhaps this was the meaning of the “never-before-done” claim made in the original article. “Towards a Study of Information Geographies” does also state that the data and findings were extremely limited by the scale at which their data collection was done. Things like estimates, more local issues of intersectionality of internet access and non-access, and other factors out of their control that would impact the data might have skewed the actual picture of digital access on a global scale. The findings did display a generalized idea of what the digital divide and information geographies look like, but it was not specific enough to recommend specific frameworks for solutions because of the generalized nature of the research done.

Something that all the articles reviewed can agree on is that the idea that the advent of the era of the internet and digital access provides an avenue of infinite internet access for all, is vastly overstated and extremely wrong for much of the global population. The internet, thus far, is not a global community, is a community of those with power, those with liveable income, those with privilege and those with the advantage of certain geographical circumstances. It is important to understand the geography of this issue, but it is also important, as seen in the other articles reviewed, to look deeper into local communities and real people to understand the reasons and circumstances behind this disparity.

8. Avenues for Future Research

When further research is conducted on this topic, local analyses are vital. There is enough information out there about disparities of global digital information geographies, there needs to be data collected from local communities in all countries, in all areas of those countries and frameworks need to be suggested and developed to fix these disparities. The scale of this level of data collection would be huge, and would take a very long time, but it would be worth it to have a database of the specific factors causing the digital divide and the lack of digital access across these information geographies.

9. Conclusion

From the close reading and analysis of other articles, here is what I can conclude: as engineers, web developers, creators of software, we bear in mind these disparities, and actively think of them as we do work in the digital world. In this era, access to digital access should be a basic human right, and the barriers to it should be removed and replaced with avenues of accessibility. As those who work with software, website design and other digital media, this is our space and community, and it should be something we work to combat in the way we design and in the way we provide access.

We should not only be studying towards information geographies and (im)mutable augmentations, but actively finding ways to improve, or even break down, the systems in place preventing digital access on a global scale.
